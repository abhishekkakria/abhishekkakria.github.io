Making the case for APAC

How a self-initiated observation became a leadership-ready international market expansion assessment at Tripleseat — researched, framed, and written end to end by one person, from a design seat.

01 · Context

A design seat, a market-sized observation

Tripleseat is a US-based event and venue management SaaS platform used by restaurants, hotels, and unique venues to sell, plan, and run private events — spanning lead capture, proposals, floorplans, and payments (PartyPay). My day job: Production Designer on the Floorplans team. A creative role, not a strategy seat — and that distinction is the point of this story.

Working inside the product daily, I kept seeing signals that Tripleseat's category problem — venues running high event volume on spreadsheets and phone calls — existed at even larger scale across APAC, with no comparable incumbent. I pitched the observation to our Creative Director. The reply:

“Put it in writing — something I can forward to leadership.”
02 · The challenge

Turn enthusiasm into a decision-ready document

The question leadership actually needed answered: should Tripleseat expand into APAC — and if so, where first, in what sequence, and validated how?

No mandate, no budget

Built entirely alongside my design workload. No analyst subscriptions, no research team — every data point sourced from public reports and verified against a second source where possible.

A skeptical, senior audience

The document would travel without me in the room. It had to anticipate objections, admit its own limitations, and read like an operator wrote it — not a fan of the idea.

A genuinely open question

APAC is not one market. Fourteen candidate countries with wildly different price tolerance, English proficiency, hotel infrastructure, and competitive density had to become one clear recommendation.

03 · The approach

A framework with evidence, not a hunch with charts

Three deliverables, one narrative: a 2-page executive summary (standalone and forwardable for time-poor leadership), a 7-page market research brief (sourced evidence on sizing, growth, competition, infrastructure), and a 15-page consolidated assessment merging the evidence with a five-phase validation framework, pilot design, financial-model structure, and risk register.

Every market was scored on the same seven lenses:

  • MICE & events market size + growth — Coherent Market Insights, Grand View Research
  • GDP per capita & price tolerance — IMF 2025
  • Labor-cost economics of the ROI case — salary benchmarks
  • Branded hotel supply & pipeline — Lodging Econometrics, Horwath HTL, Hotelivate
  • English proficiency — EF EPI 2025
  • Payment rails for PartyPay — UPI, PromptPay, QRIS, DuitNow, VietQR
  • Competitive density — Cvent, iVvy, Social Tables, Prismm
04 · The framework

Fourteen markets, three tiers, one sequence

Tier 1 — Enter first

Australia / NZ · Singapore

English-native, chain-dense, high willingness to pay. Australia offers a beatable incumbent (iVvy) with documented onboarding complaints; Singapore's MICE demand is literally government-subsidised — BEiS co-funds up to ~50% of qualifying event costs.

Tier 2 — Build toward

India · Southeast Asia

The largest prize with the hardest go-to-market. Enormous event volume and record hotel pipelines, but pricing, relationships, and compliance demand real localisation before entry.

Tier 3 — Monitor only

Japan · S. Korea · Greater China

Big on paper, structurally slow or inaccessible: Japan's venue base is overwhelmingly independents; Korea faces demographic headwinds on social events; China is effectively closed to a US SaaS entrant.

Recommendation: sequence, don't scatter — prove the model where conditions are friendliest, fund the harder markets with the learning.

06 · The differentiator

I argued against my own strongest market

A banquet or events coordinator in India earns roughly ₹3–6 lakh (~$3,500–7,000) a year. Venues can simply hire more admin staff cheaply — which quietly breaks the classic “software replaces labor” ROI pitch, even in the highest event-volume market on earth. I put this in the brief deliberately: it pushes back on the document's own most exciting number, and that is what makes the rest of it credible.

So the value proposition moved — rebuilt around what cheap labor can't do:

Revenue, not cost

Lead capture and response speed — won bookings scale with volume regardless of wages.

Coordination at scale

Multi-property visibility and double-booking prevention across high-volume calendars.

Owner-level control

Portfolio reporting no amount of extra admin headcount produces.

Sharper targeting

Aim the India GTM at chains and multi-venue operators — not independent banquet halls.

07 · From insight to action

A pilot leadership could actually run

Australia test

  • Positioning — venue-first system for selling more private events, set directly against iVvy's documented onboarding and reporting complaints.
  • Channels — LinkedIn ABM, paid search, hospitality associations, hotel trade groups, webinars.
  • Metrics — CTR → demo conversion → SQL rate, target-account engagement, pipeline value, sales-cycle feedback.

Singapore test

  • Positioning — convert BEiS-fuelled corporate event demand faster with modern venue sales and event workflows.
  • Channels — LinkedIn ABM, paid search, the SECB / business-events ecosystem, executive content.
  • Metrics — demo conversion, SQL rate, account quality, buyer interviews, competitive win/loss feedback.
Financial model: structure, not theatre. The assessment defines the TAM/SAM/SOM, CAC, support-burden and payments-upside model for leadership to populate with internal data — and deliberately refuses to invent revenue projections it can't support.
08 · Pre-empting the room

Objections answered before they were raised

“We sell to venues — not weddings or corporate planners.”

Agreed, and the paper never suggests otherwise. The customer in every tier is the venue; weddings and MICE are the demand flowing through venues — deliberately kept in the venue framing.

“Why not keep compounding the US and UK?”

Not either/or. The assessment argues sequencing, not reallocation — a bounded, measurable pilot alongside the core business, with kill criteria defined up front.

“How much of this can we trust?”

The document includes its own ‘Honest limitations’ section: where estimates diverge, where public data thins out, and which assumptions require internal numbers to resolve.

09 · Outcome

From side observation to the leadership table

Forwarded upward

The Creative Director shared the assessment package with senior leadership — exactly the outcome the “put it in writing” brief asked for.

Authored end to end

All three documents — summary, brief, and 15-page assessment — researched, structured, written, and designed by me alone.

A repositioned profile

The work moved my profile internally from ‘the designer’ toward someone who thinks in markets, evidence, and GTM sequence.

Honest framing: market entry is a multi-quarter decision and the pilot is leadership's call to green-light. What this case study evidences is the PMM craft — the research, the framework, the positioning, and a document senior stakeholders chose to pass upward.
