Part I

The market - research, competition, and who buys

01 • Executive summary

A niche specialist in an underserved gap

SolarVista3D is a B2B studio providing photorealistic 3D rendering, planning-grade photomontages, and cinematic animation to EPCs, solar and wind developers, IPPs, and power-infrastructure companies. Renewable projects are typically presented as CAD drawings and engineering documents - technically accurate, but illegible to the investors, planning authorities, and communities who decide their fate. SolarVista3D translates engineering into evidence: visuals that win approvals, strengthen bids, and accelerate stakeholder buy-in.

The studio is founder-operated: segmentation, pricing, outbound, SEO, negotiation, and production delivery are all run by one person. That makes this a full-funnel GTM case study - with no marketing team, no inherited brand equity, and every outcome directly attributable to the strategy chosen.

02 • Industry research

Renewables boomed; visualization didn't keep up

Global solar and wind build out has expanded at record pace, and every utility-scale project passes through the same communication chokepoints: planning consent, landowner and community consultation, tender submissions, and investor approval. Each is a moment where a non-technical audience must understand a technical proposal - and where a photomontage or render does what a site plan cannot.

Permitting pressure

Planning authorities increasingly expect visual-impact evidence - accurate, viewpoint-verified photomontages, not marketing art.

Capital competition

Developers compete for finance; investor decks with credible visuals communicate feasibility faster than engineering annexes.

Community consent

Local opposition is a leading cause of delay. Showing a community exactly what a project will look like de-risks the timeline.

Yet few visualization studios understand tracker systems, terrain modelling, substations, access roads, and electrical infrastructure. Generic arch-viz shops make solar farms look wrong; the specialists who don't are rare. That gap is the business.

03 • Competitive analysis

A polarized supplier landscape - and the space between

The competitive field splits into two poles. At the bottom: low-cost domestic freelancers competing purely on price, with inconsistent quality and no sector knowledge. At the top: a handful of premium international studios competing on trust and turnaround reliability, priced accordingly. There is very little in between.

The positioning: production quality close to the premium tier, at a founder-operated cost base, sold with the discipline of a specialist - not the desperation of a generalist freelancer.

Low-cost freelancers

Compete on price

Cheap, fast, generic. No renewables domain knowledge; quality risk on planning-grade work. Sets the price floor SolarVista3D refuses to chase.

SolarVista3D

Compete on specialization

Engineering-aware production (GIS, CAD, terrain), premium-adjacent quality, founder economics - priced by market, never by desperation.

Premium int'l studios

Compete on trust

Established brands with agency overheads and agency prices. The benchmark for quality - and the reference point EUR pricing signals parity with.

Part II

The brand - positioning, identity, service, and stack

05 • Brand strategy

Specialist, not studio-for-hire

The brand communicates three things in order: technical expertise, trust, and premium quality. Vision: become the leading renewable-energy visualization partner. Mission: help renewable-energy companies communicate projects with clarity through technically accurate, visually compelling imagery.

Every brand decision reinforces the specialist claim - the studio talks about tracker systems and viewpoint verification, not "stunning renders." Content demonstrates domain expertise rather than displaying pretty pictures, because the buyer is an engineer or a proposal manager, not a design enthusiast.

06 • Visual identity

Designed to be trusted by engineers

The identity uses a minimal aesthetic - clean typography, strong whitespace, restrained color, professional tone - deliberately closer to an engineering consultancy than a creative agency. The renders are the color; the brand stays quiet around them. Rate cards, proposals, and the website all share this system, so every artifact a prospect touches signals the same thing: this studio works the way infrastructure clients work.

07 • Service blueprint & production workflow

From CAD file to consent-ready visual

Services span utility-scale solar visualization, rooftop and commercial renders, floating solar, agrivoltaics, BESS facilities, planning photomontages, proposal graphics, investor presentation assets, construction-phasing graphics, and 30–90 second cinematic animations. All run through one production spine:

  1. Discovery & briefing - project goals, audience, submission requirements.
  2. Data review - CAD, DWG, KMZ, GIS layers, and project documentation.
  3. Terrain & context - accurate topography and environmental surroundings.
  4. Asset modelling - PV arrays, trackers, turbines, substations, access roads.
  5. Materials & lighting - physically plausible surfaces, sun studies.
  6. Rendering - V-Ray production output in HD/4K.
  7. Photomontage & post - viewpoint-matched composites, color grading.
  8. Delivery - presentation-ready assets packaged for the end use.

Part III

The engine - website, SEO, content, funnel, and pricing

09 • Website UX & SEO strategy

A lead-generation platform, not a portfolio

The website (solarvista3d.com) is built as a conversion asset: service pages mapped to what buyers search for ("3D visualization for EPCs", "solar farm rendering", "investor-ready renewable visuals"), geographic coverage signals for India, Europe, Australia and the Gulf, and clear enquiry paths - rather than a gallery that assumes the visitor already knows what they need.

The SEO work started with a diagnostic, not a content sprint. Search Console showed the site already earned periodic organic traffic with an above-average click-through rate at an average ranking position of ~10. That reframed the priority entirely:

Diagnose before you optimize: strong CTR at position 10 means the constraint is ranking position, not traffic volume. On-page and authority work to close the gap to positions 3–5 beats chasing new keyword territory - a more capital-efficient sequence for a solo operator.
10 • Content & LinkedIn strategy

Expertise as the warm-up act for outbound

Content runs LinkedIn-first: founder-profile commentary and posts in the EPC/renewables niche, educational content, and before-and-after comparisons that demonstrate what planning-grade visualization actually changes. The strategic role is sequencing - content warms outbound contacts before the direct email arrives, shortening the trust-building cycle that cold outreach to infrastructure buyers otherwise requires. Commenting from the personal profile was deliberately prioritized as the higher-impact growth lever over page-follow mechanics.

11 • Sales funnel & CRM process

A structured pipeline, not ad hoc follow-up

Outreach follows a defined sequence - research → personalized email → follow-up → qualification → proposal - aimed at proposal managers, BD heads, project managers, and directors. A structured database of ~150 contacts across ~70 companies supports segment-aware outreach: Indian EPCs, international EPCs, and developer/IPP contacts each get tailored subject lines and value propositions, matched to the right rate card.

12 • Pricing strategy

Pricing as a segmentation tool

Rather than one global rate card, pricing was built to encode willingness-to-pay, competitive intensity, and currency risk per geography - and packaged as polished EUR and INR rate-card PDFs, so one outreach motion routes any prospect to the correct currency and tier without ad hoc quoting.

MarketCurrencyStructureStrategic logic
IndiaINRFour tiers, defined pricing floor, volume-discount logicPrice-sensitive but high-volume; tiering serves small EPCs and large infrastructure players from one rate card without underpricing either
EuropeEUREuropean market ratesPricing must signal parity with local studios, not an "offshore discount" - underpricing would undermine the premium positioning referrals depend on

The discipline that mattered most: refusing to underprice international work to win it. India-market interest is treated as top-of-funnel volume and proof of capability; European and Australian accounts are margin-accretive, reference-building relationships - priced for the trust premium they carry. A key rule: pricing follows the project's end-market, not the contracting office's location.
Part IV

The work - engagement types and demand generation in practice

13 • Engagement types

Five project archetypes, one delivered anchor

The service portfolio is organized around five representative engagement types - each with a distinct buyer, deliverable, and decision it supports:

Utility-scale solar (50 MW+)

Full-site visualization: terrain-accurate array layouts, substations, access roads. Supports planning approval and investor presentations for developers and IPPs.

Commercial rooftop

Building-accurate rooftop system renders for C&I sales teams - helping EPCs close commercial clients who need to see the install before signing.

Floating solar

Water-body visualization with anchoring and environmental context - a fast-growing niche where generic studios reliably get the engineering wrong.

BESS facilities

Battery storage site visuals for permitting and community communication - critical as BESS projects face rising local scrutiny.

Agrivoltaics

Dual-use farm visualizations showing crops and arrays coexisting - the exact image planning authorities and landowners need to approve novel land use.

Delivered: wind photomontages (Germany)

The studio's first wind-sector engagement: five planning-grade photomontages across two sites - a forest windpark and a Brandenburg site, both on Nordex N175 turbines - for a returning German engineering client, with a follow-on two-video CG flythrough package quoted at a €1,700 base.

Why the wind project matters strategically: it came from expanding an existing relationship into a new service line, not from new-logo prospecting - proof that account expansion deserves as much deliberate strategy as acquisition.

14 • Event-led demand generation

BES 2026: buying a concentrated pipeline

Exhibiting at the Bharat Electricity Summit (New Delhi) was a deliberate bet away from inbound-and-cold-email dependence: direct visibility with EPCs and power infrastructure buyers, in person. The event produced a set of qualified leads - including LC Infra, Advait Group, Manikaran Power, and Alfanar Projects - giving the studio a concentrated domestic EPC pipeline to work against the freshly built four-tier pricing structure and bilingual rate cards.

Part V

Looking forward - economics, roadmap, and lessons

15 • Financial model

Unit economics: structure, not theatre

Revenue comes from one-time visualization projects, bundled proposal packages, animation upsells, and long-term EPC partnerships. Pricing reflects business value - what a won consent or a strengthened bid is worth - not rendering hours. The model is deliberately presented as structure rather than invented projections:

Revenue mix logic

India tiers drive volume and utilization; EUR/USD projects drive margin. Animation packages raise average deal size within existing accounts.

Cost base

Founder-operated: no payroll, no studio overhead. The structural advantage that makes premium-adjacent quality viable at competitive rates.

Growth lever

Repeat accounts and referrals over new logos - a closed international client is worth multiples of its first invoice in references and expansion scope.

17 • Lessons learned

What building it actually taught

Positioning is defended with pricing, not messaging

Saying "premium studio" means nothing; charging European rates in Europe is what makes the claim credible to a buyer comparing quotes.

Existing relationships are a growth channel

The wind vertical opened from inside an account, not from prospecting. Account expansion deserves deliberate strategy, not leftover attention.

A missed lead is a process gap, not a lost deal

The Octopus Australia recovery worked because the response was structural (multi-threading) rather than emotional (chasing one contact).

Diagnose before you optimize

The Search Console audit prevented a wasted "more content" cycle by proving the real constraint was ranking position, not traffic.

"Indian EPCs show interest without converting at the same rate as international clients - which is why Germany and Australia traction has become the credibility anchor for every other conversation."
